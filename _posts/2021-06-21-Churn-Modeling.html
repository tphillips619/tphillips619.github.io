Churn Modeling¶

Customers engage in transactions at different frequencies and can churn at any time. How do you differentiate churn in high-frequency customers from low-frequency customers with longer inter-purchase periods?

  1. Recency, Frequency, Monetary (RFM) Approach
  • Discretize the customers into predefined bins regarding their Recency, Frequency, and Monetary value. Create different rules for each bin.
  1. Buy Till You Defect (BTYD) Approach
  • The BTYD approach treats the transaction history as the product of two processes: the propensity to purchase and the propensity to churn.

RFM¶

Lets create a customer dataframe and begin aggregating customer level statistics.

customer_df = tx.groupby('id')['date'].agg(['count','min','max'])
customer_df.columns = ['frequency', 'first_purchase_date', 
                       'last_purchase_date']

customer_df['Frequency Group'] = pd.cut(customer_df['frequency'], 
                          bins=[0,1,10,np.inf],
                          labels=['1', '2-10','10+'])

customer_df['days_since_last_purchase'] = (customer_df['last_purchase_date'].max()
                                           - customer_df['last_purchase_date']).dt.days

customer_df['Recency Group'] = pd.cut(customer_df['days_since_last_purchase'],
                         bins=[0,30,90,np.inf],
                         labels=['<30','30-90','90+'])

customer_df['Monetary Total'] = tx.groupby('id')['monetary_total'].sum()

A knowledgeable product manager could create different rules for each Frequency Group that define when a customer has churned (i.e. a customer with 10+ transactions has churned if they have not transacted in the past 30 days). This approach is effective, but requires managing (and re-adjusting) the bin edges. Also, there will be customers who bounce back and forth between the churned and alive groups because their purchase hiatus is close to the pre-defined bin widths.

BTYD¶

Another approach is to keep the definition of churn constant across all customers, but assign a probability alive score conditioned on the frequency and recency for the whole customer base. I'll use the python lifetimes implementation, although the R implementation of BTYD has support for more models from the literature, is better maintained, and is well-documented.

from lifetimes.utils import summary_data_from_transaction_data
from lifetimes import BetaGeoFitter

summary = summary_data_from_transaction_data(tx, 'id', 'date')#,
                                             #observation_period_end='1997-12-31')

bgf = BetaGeoFitter(penalizer_coef=0.01)
bgf.fit(summary['frequency'], 
        summary['recency'], 
        summary['T'])

print(bgf)

A BetaGeoFitter takes the customer level stats of frequency and recency as well as the time interval for the entire series (x, t_x, and T in the literature) and obtains 4 population wide parameters:

alpha and r: the parameters for the Gamma distribution that model customer transaction rates

a and b: the parameters for the Beta distribution that model customer churn

With these 3 customer level parameters, and 4 population wide parameters, we can calculate the probability that the customer is alive at any given time with the formula:

In [8]:
summary['P_alive'] = bgf.conditional_probability_alive(summary['frequency'], 
                                                       summary['recency'], 
                                                       summary['T'])

The P_alive score is best understood when visualized with a set of customers with different purchase frequencies.

The most notable difference is that the P_alive score drops much faster for higher frequency customers than for lower frequency customers.

A slightly less noticeable difference is that the P_alive score immediately after a purchase starts low and increases after each purchase a customer makes. The more purchases a customer makes with a company, the less likely they are to churn.

What Next?¶

List of At-Risk Customers¶

The BD/NBD model shines when identifying higher frequency customers who have not crossed the threshold into a different bin of the RFM approach. A P_alive score for every customer enables the creation of lists of customers who are "at-risk" of churning. This is especially useful when combined with the customer's monetary value.

Generation of Customer Segments¶

When combined with other customer information (e.g. demographics), the P_alive score can help generate segments of customers who are worth targeting. Use a calibration/holdout set in order to obtain residuals for the P_alive predictions. Think against the gradient, fit a parsimonious parametric model on the residuals, and interpret the results!

Conclusion¶

The problem of understanding repeat transactions in a continous time non-contractual setting appears in e-commerce, hospital readmissions, blogging, and even liveness for nodes in a communication network. They all have the same underlying problem of users interacting with you at different rates and then churning at some point in the future.

Customer churn is unobserved. Even if you were able to directly ask the customer if they plan to make another purchase, their answer may not be reliable because circumstances may change after they provide the answer. This makes it a challenging thing to measure, but a great candidate to model with a Bayesian approach.
